## The problem

By 2021 the monolith that had carried the company for five years was the
bottleneck for every team: a single deploy queue, a shared database with
40+ implicit contracts, and p99 latency that doubled every quarter of
growth. Six product teams shipped through one release train.

## Constraints

- Zero downtime — payments flowed through the same runtime.
- No feature freeze longer than two weeks, ever.
- The team that would run the platform was the team building it: twelve
  engineers, four of whom had never operated a service in production.

## The approach

We refused the big-bang rewrite. Instead: put a gateway in front of the
monolith first, move traffic one route at a time, and let every extracted
service prove itself under real load before the next one started.

```go
// The strangler route table — boring on purpose.
// Every migration was one line, one review, one rollback path.
routes := gateway.Table{
    "/v1/orders":   backend.Service("orders-svc"),
    "/v1/payments": backend.Service("payments-svc"),
    "/v1/*":        backend.Legacy(monolith), // shrinking every sprint
}
```

## Architecture

Envoy at the edge, gRPC between services, one Postgres per service with
change-data-capture feeding the pipeline. The controversial call was
keeping a shared identity service instead of per-service auth — it saved
four months and nobody has regretted it.

## Outcome

- 40M requests/day through the gateway, p99 38ms (from 480ms).
- Deploys went from one train per day to 30+ independent releases.
- Zero rewrites since: the 2021 architecture absorbed three product
  pivots without structural change.

## What I would do differently

Start the observability work two quarters earlier. We instrumented as we
extracted; instrumenting *before* extracting would have cut the two
scariest migrations in half.
